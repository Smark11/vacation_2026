Chapter One · Iceland · Jun 25–30

Where the sun won't set

Reykjavík at eleven at night in late June looks like seven o'clock everywhere else — a low, butter-yellow sun pulling rectangles of light across corrugated-iron houses painted oxblood and lichen-green. The air carries a faint sulfur from the showers and roasted lamb from someone's grill. Children are still on bikes in 101; down at Grandi harbour, fishing boats with chipped paint nudge the dock; up Laugavegur, the bookshop windows glow at midnight. The city looks like a town and acts like a capital.

The South Coast & Reykjavík

Four nights, two bases: the first two on the South Coast — close to the great waterfalls and the Westman Islands ferry — then two in Reykjavík, the world's northernmost capital. Rent a small SUV or 2WD at Keflavík: paved Ring Road 1 carries you everywhere on this itinerary, and the F-roads to the highland interior open only later in summer.

History & context

Reykjavík — smoky bay — was founded in 874 AD by the Norse settler Ingólfr Arnarson and remained a few sod-roofed cottages for nine centuries. In 1944, while the rest of Europe was at war, Iceland voted itself a republic from Denmark. Everything in today's 240,000-person capital runs on geothermal and hydroelectric power: the showers smell of sulfur because the hot tap is literally hot-spring water piped from Mosfellsdalur. The entire country sits astride the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — the only place on Earth where you can stand between two diverging continental plates above sea level. The rift makes the magma rise easily; the rising magma makes the geothermal; the geothermal melts the glaciers; the glacial outflow makes the waterfalls. Iceland is a single, coherent geological argument.

 More of the island
The story of Friðheimar

Off the Golden Circle in Reykholt, the Knútur-Helena Friðheimar family grows ~370 tonnes of tomatoes a year in heated glass — about 18% of all the tomatoes in Iceland — at a latitude where outdoor agriculture is basically impossible. A 200 m borehole next door delivers 95°C water to warm the greenhouses; lamps run 18 hours a day in winter to mimic Mediterranean sun; ~1,200 imported bumblebees do all the pollination, each one visiting up to 2,000 flowers a day.

The lunch is served at long communal tables among the vines: tomato soup with unlimited refills, sourdough, ravioli, a Bloody Mary if you want one. Predicted nine-year-old verdict: "I didn't know I liked tomato soup."

A field guide to the puffin

Half the world's Atlantic puffins — eight to ten million birds — breed on Iceland's cliffs every summer. You'll be there at the height of the chick-feeding season. They are likely to be the most magical encounter of the trip; here's everything to know.

Fratercula arctica

The Old Norse name lundi means "little brother of the friars" — a nod to the black-and-white robe. Puffins live most of their lives out on the open North Atlantic, returning to land only to breed. They mate for life, live twenty to thirty years, and recognise their partner each spring by a small rub of the beak. They are extraordinary at almost everything except looking dignified on take-off.

Things to know (for the small biologist)
Dive depth
60 metres deep

Pursuit-divers with wings as flippers. They can stay underwater for ~30 seconds at a time, chasing capelin and sand-eels.

Wing-beats
~400 per minute

The tiny wings beat about as fast as a hummingbird's — top speed 55 mph. Looks chaotic; perfectly efficient.

The beak
Up to 12 fish at once

Backward-pointing barbs on the upper palate hold a row of fish while the puffin keeps catching more — a clothesline of dinner.

The colour
Only for summer

The bright orange beak and feet are breeding plumage. After breeding the colourful outer beak plates shed and turn a duller grey.

The burrow
1 metre deep, dug by claw

One egg, one chick (a puffling) per year. Both parents take turns warming and feeding, swapping every few hours.

Lifespan
25–30 years

Couples re-find each other at the same burrow every spring. They preen each other's faces — "billing" — for hours.

A story for the small reader · the Puffling Patrol

On the island of Heimaey (Vestmannaeyjar) every August, baby puffins fledge from their burrows for the first time and try to fly out to sea — but the village's electric lights confuse them, and hundreds end up lost in town instead of out on the water. So the children of Heimaey go on a Puffling Patrol: every night with cardboard boxes and torches, hunting for lost pufflings on porches and under cars, taking them home to be weighed and tagged, then carrying them to the cliffs at sunset and gently throwing them into the sea breeze so they can soar at last. Over a thousand pufflings get rescued every August. It is, by national consensus, the best job a nine-year-old can have.

À table · the food of Iceland

A pantry forged by isolation — lamb, fish, rye, dairy. For centuries every ingredient came from the sea, the mountainside, or a barrel buried in geothermal earth. A small Icelandic quirk

Icelanders don't have surnames — they have patronymics. A man named Jón who fathers a son named Ólafur produces Ólafur Jónsson ("Jón's son"); his daughter is Katrín Jónsdóttir ("Jón's daughter"). Marriage doesn't change names; siblings only share a last name because they share a father. The phone book is therefore alphabetized by first name — because last names don't run in families and aren't useful as a sort key. Since 1925, first names must come from an official register (the Mannanafnaskrá) or be approved by the Mannanafnanefnd, the Naming Committee. This is why your tour guide will introduce herself just as Anna, and why the prime minister is "Katrín" on national television.

🌍
For the small geologist

At Þingvellir on Day 2 you will walk down into a literal crack in the Earth — the Almannagjá rift. On your left is the North American continent; on your right is Europe. The two plates are pulling apart from each other at about 2.5 centimetres a year — exactly the speed your fingernails grow. By the time you are forty, the two continents will be roughly a metre further apart than they are right now. You are watching a very slow goodbye between two halves of the world.

Voices & the local rhythm
What you'll hear on the street

The most-used Icelandic phrase is þetta reddast ("theh-tah RED-ast") — roughly, "it will work out in the end." It is said about everything from a snowstorm to a soup. The thank-you is takk fyrir ("tahk FEER-eer"). The phonebook lists people by first name — because surnames don't run in families — so the whole nation is, in a way, on a first-name basis with itself. Children's books rhyme in Old Norse practically unchanged for a thousand years; a nine-year-old in Reykjavík can read the same poems the Vikings did.

A moment to seek

You're in a RIB off Heimaey, engine cut, rocking gently below a cliff that goes straight up into the sky. The air is loud — thousands of puffins wheeling overhead, more bobbing on the swell an arm's length away, orange feet paddling, painted beaks turning to look at you. Your nine-year-old has stopped talking entirely. The guide says something about how these birds spend the whole winter alone on the open ocean and come back here to find the same partner, at the same burrow, every single summer. A puffin clatters off the water right beside the boat. You will replay this single minute in your head for the rest of your life.

Chapter Two · Cinque Terre · Jul 1–5

Five villages, one little train

You wake to the sound of waves slapping the seawall, and through the half-shuttered window comes the smell of salt, espresso, and warm focaccia. By nine the Cinque Terre Express is disgorging day-trippers in lemon-yellow hats onto the Fegina platform. The houses climb the cliff in pastel stacks — apricot, mustard, terracotta — held in place, it seems, by laundry lines. By dusk the heat fades, the swallows wheel above the bell tower of San Giovanni Battista, and a glass of cold Vermentino tastes like the place itself: mineral, sun-warmed, faintly briny.

History & context

The five villages were carved by Ligurian peasants who terraced these near-vertical cliffs with an estimated 7,000 km of dry-stone walls — the muretti a secco — beginning in the 11th century, to plant the vines that produce Cinque Terre DOC and its rare passito cousin, Sciacchetrà. For centuries the villages were reachable only by boat or mule path; the railway tunnel opened in 1874 finally connected them to the world. UNESCO listed the coast as a World Heritage Site in 1997 and Italy made it a National Park in 1999 — which is why every paying visitor today underwrites the same fragile terraces.

For the small chef

Real pesto is always made with a mortar and pestle (mortaio e pestello) — never a blender. The metal blades heat and bruise the basil leaves, turning the sauce brown in minutes. The slow stone grinding releases the oils gently and keeps the colour bright green for hours. If you ever see brown pesto, the kitchen used a blender.

Practical tips

Getting in: Train via La Spezia Centrale (1 h from Pisa, 3 h from Milan); change to the Cinque Terre Express. The villages themselves are car-free — drive only as far as the La Spezia or Levanto parking garages.

For the small mathematician

The terraces under the Cinque Terre vineyards are held up by muretti a secco — dry-stone walls — built by hand a thousand years ago, no mortar, just rock balanced on rock. If you stretched all of them out end to end they would reach about seven thousand kilometres, far enough to cross the Atlantic Ocean. From above, the cliffs look like they are wearing a knitted sweater.

Voices & the local rhythm
What you'll hear on the harbour

The villages speak Zeneise, a dialect of Ligurian that sounds more like Provençal than Italian — cut-off endings, rolled rs, words like tröfie, fugassa, çimma. After 1 PM the greeting shifts from buongiorno to buon pomeriggio, and the locals will gently correct you. At dusk the whole village walks the harbour together — the passeggiata — older than the train tunnel under it. You'll fall in behind a grandfather and find your own rhythm.

A moment to seek

It's the 7:32 PM boat back from Vernazza to Monterosso. Your nine-year-old has salt-dried curls and a half-eaten gelato. The sun is at exactly that low angle that turns every pastel façade into a Pantone swatch. The boat lifts and falls in its slow rhythm; an older Ligurian man across from you sees your kid yawning and says, with a fingertip kiss, "buon appetito al sole" — the sun is eating. You will think about this ninety seconds for years.

Chapter Three · Sestri Levante · Jul 5–7

Two bays, one fairy tale

Just up the coast from the Cinque Terre, the crowds thin and the cliffs soften into something gentler: a small headland with a bay on each side, pastel fishermen's houses pulled right down to the sand, and a town that actually exhales. Sestri Levante is where Ligurians themselves come to swim — calmer, sandier, and slower than the five villages, with gelato in the caruggi and a carousel turning on the promenade at dusk. After four days of stairs and day-trippers, it is a soft place to land.

History & context

Sestri grew on a tombolo — a sandy isthmus tying a rocky islet (now "the Isola") to the mainland — which is why it has two beaches back to back. A fishing and shipbuilding town for centuries, it became a discreet seaside retreat in the 1800s; in 1835 the Danish storyteller Hans Christian Andersen stayed here and was so charmed that the western bay took the name Baia delle Favole — the Bay of Fables. The town still throws an Andersen storytelling festival every summer. The eastern cove kept the name a fisherman gives a sheltered anchorage: Baia del Silenzio, the Bay of Silence.

Chapter Four · Lake Como · Jul 7–10

Como, impossibly elegant

If Cinque Terre is the Mediterranean turned up to eleven, Lake Como is its inverse — a long, hushed Y of cold mountain water with cypress and oleander reflected in it, and a hundred years of villas pretending not to notice each other. The mornings in Varenna smell of espresso and cool stone; the ferry whistle skips off the water; old men in straw hats argue about football in Piazza San Giorgio. By afternoon the breeze called the breva climbs the lake from the south, kite-surfers appear off Domaso, and someone with too much money is having lunch on a teak deck off Bellagio.

History & context

Carved by the last Ice Age into a 410-meter-deep glacial trough, Lake Como has been a summer retreat since Pliny the Younger wrote about his two villas here in the 1st century AD. The 18th and 19th centuries scattered some of Europe's loveliest ville di delizia along its shores — Carlotta, Melzi, Balbianello, Serbelloni — built by aristocrats, polished by botanists, and saved (often) by FAI, Italy's National Trust. Today the western shore is the glamour line; Varenna and the Lecco arm keep more of the village rhythm.

Finale · Milan & Home · Jun 30 + Jul 10

Milan negotiates

Milan does not seduce; Milan negotiates. The light is industrial in the morning, golden by 6 PM, neon by 9. Trams the color of old paper rattle past the Duomo. Men in suits stand at the bar for a 90-second espresso, no sugar, no chat. Then the Galleria's marble floors throw back the chandeliers, the rooftop of the cathedral lifts you above a forest of pinnacles, and the city's other voice — the one in tailoring, in design, in 700-year-old engineering — clears its throat. By aperitivo at the Navigli canals, the day softens. There is a tomato risotto somewhere with your name on it.

History & context

Founded by Celts ~600 BC, made Western Roman capital in 286 AD under Diocletian, ruined by Barbarossa in 1162, and rebuilt by the Visconti and Sforza dukes who started the Duomo in 1386 (it was finished in 1965 — yes, 1965). Napoleon crowned himself King of Italy here in 1805; the Austrians ruled until 1859; the Fascists rebuilt Milano Centrale as a marble monument in 1931. After WWII it became Italy's design, fashion, and finance capital — Pirelli, Olivetti, La Scala, Armani. The Duomo and the Last Supper are the twin cultural engines; everything else is shopping, design, or aperitivo.

À table · the food of Milan

A cuisine of butter, marrow, and saffron. Where most of Italy cooks in olive oil, Milan cooks in butter. Aperitivo was invented here in 1860, and is still done best.

Aperitivo at six is sacred, and every bar puts food out — the city pours into the streets. It is, in three hours, the most civilised hour anywhere.

A moment to seek

You're on the Duomo rooftop just before closing — late light, fewer people, the spires going from white to coral. A flock of starlings sweeps past at eye level. Your kid touches the warm marble of a flying buttress and asks how they got the saints up here. Below you the city hums; somewhere a No. 2 tram clangs. You don't answer right away. You just stand there and let the question hang against the Madonnina, who has watched over Milan since 1774, gold leaf flaking quietly into the smog.

What to bring home

A trip becomes a memory by what you carry forward — a flavour, a phrase, a habit, a small ritual that survives the suitcase. Here are the things to plant, so that sixteen days in Iceland and Italy stay alive long after the flights home.

In your suitcase
  • A small bottle of Ligurian (Riviera) olive oil — delicate and sweet, pressed on the coast you just left. Use it sparingly through the autumn; finish it by Thanksgiving.
  • A panettone from Marchesi — sealed in foil for the flight home. Open Christmas Eve.
  • A jar of Ligurian pesto from Forno Ulisse — the real kind, mortar-and-pestle, made yesterday. Eat it on toast the night you land.
  • An Icelandic lopapeysa from Handprjónasamband — the Hand-Knitting Association of Iceland. Your sweater will be one of only a few thousand made each year, each by a different Icelandic grandmother.
  • A small bottle of brennivín for the family bar (the bottle is the gift), and an Icelandic chocolate bar from Omnom for the small reader.
In your hands · small daily rituals
  • The 6 PM aperitivo — even on a Tuesday in February. A spritz, an olive, a few minutes before dinner is started.
  • A passeggiata after supper — a slow walk together, just to walk. The Italians have a word for it; we don't have to call it anything.
  • The cinnamon-bun Saturday — a snúður on Saturday morning. We can recreate it; the smell from Brauð & Co should stay with you for a year.
  • The Strokkur countdown — count to five with your daughter at every waterfall and geyser you see from now on.
In your head · phrases to keep
  • Þetta reddast. Icelandic for "it will work out." For the flat tire, the lost glove, the bad week at school.
  • Buon appetito al sole. Ligurian for "the sun is eating" — what you say at golden hour.
  • La dolce vita. For when life is good. Use it sincerely; the Italians do.

After

The flight home is a daytime nonstop into Newark. You'll leave the lake in the late morning, ride the train down to Malpensa, and be home that night. The hardest part of any trip is not the journey out; it is the morning after the journey home, when the world looks slightly the same but you have been changed by it.
