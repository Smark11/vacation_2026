Ending the trip, your way

Verona is the book’s recommended finale — but it sits east of the lake, so it’s a sight, not a shortcut to the plane. Here is everything that lies the other way, west toward Malpensa: where to spend the last night (or grab a stop on the way out), and what’s worth a day even though it’s the wrong direction for the flight. The single rule that governs them all — there is no train into Malpensa that skips Milan: every route changes at Milano Centrale onto the Malpensa Express (~51 min, every ~30 min). For the 4 PM flight on Jul 10, aim to be on one by noon.

On the way out

The last night, heading toward the gate

Four ways to spend the final night that all shorten the airport run versus Verona — from the absolute-lowest-stress (a Milan night, an hour from the gate) to the simply lovely (one more evening on the lake at Desenzano).

On the way out · ~1 h to MXP — simplest of all

A second Milan night

This one isn't about discovery — you already land in Milan on the 30th of June, so the city won't be a stranger. It's about giving a 4 PM flight room to breathe. Sleep beside Milano Centrale and the airport is a single Malpensa Express away: roughly fifty minutes, trains leaving every half hour, so you can amble out around half past eleven and still arrive with time to spare. And a second night quietly buys back the big kid-headline sights you almost certainly didn't reach the first time round — the ones worth doubling back for.

  • Museo Leonardo da Vinci — Italy's largest science museum: hands-on models of Leonardo's machines, a space-and-transport hall, and a real submarine, the Enrico Toti, you can walk straight through; budget a good three hours.
  • The Duomo rooftop — a walk up among the spires and pinnacles, the whole pale forest of marble at arm's length.
  • Castello Sforzesco — a proper turreted fortress to roam, with the MUBA children's museum close by.

Getting there & the airport run: from Peschiera it's about an hour direct to Milano Centrale; then a single Malpensa Express (~51 min, every ~30 min) carries you to the airport — leave around 11:30 AM for a 4 PM flight and you're there with a buffer. Tip: book a hotel by Milano Centrale so you can walk to the Express, not a generic "near-airport" one.

On the way out · ~1¾–2 h to MXP — the all-rounder

Brescia ★ OUR PICK

Barely twenty minutes west of Peschiera, and the train runs straight there, Brescia is the rare place that works equally well as a day out or as a quiet last night before the airport. This is Roman history a nine-year-old can actually clamber over: the Brixia archaeological park lays out a Capitolium temple and an open Roman theatre, with the city's beloved bronze Winged Victory presiding over it all. Next door, the UNESCO Santa Giulia museum hides a whole convent's worth of mosaics and frescoes — and, crucially, knows how to keep a child looking. Above the rooftops, a proper hilltop castle waits with ramparts to roam. And in July, all that cool museum stone is its own quiet reward.

  • Brixia archaeological park — a real Roman temple, theatre and the bronze Winged Victory, out in the open to walk among.
  • Santa Giulia museum — a "Geronimo Stilton: Brescia Musei Adventures" hunt for ages 6+, plus an "art in a box" activity case you request at the door.
  • Castello di Brescia — a big hilltop castle with ramparts to roam and long views back over the city.

Getting there & the airport run: From Peschiera it's a direct train, only about 20 minutes west. For the airport, Brescia → Milano Centrale runs ~36 min, then the Malpensa Express ~51 min — roughly 1h45–2h all in: somewhere genuinely new, and a shorter run than Verona.

On the way out · ~1h50 to MXP — the scenic splurge

Bergamo

The prettiest of the bunch, and the one that asks to be saved for last. A little funicular — around €2.60 there and back — climbs out of the lower town and lifts you up to the Città Alta, the walled city crowning the hill, and the ride alone is enough to make a nine-year-old press their nose to the glass. Up top the streets are old and golden and made for wandering, the kind of place where an evening unspools into a slow passeggiata and a gelato that somehow tastes better at altitude. It is compact and atmospheric, the whole hilltop walkable in a single unhurried evening.

  • The UNESCO Venetian Walls — a 6-km ring of ramparts with big views and plenty of room to run.
  • Piazza Vecchia & the Campanone — the Contarini fountain to circle, and a bell tower you can climb by stairs or ride up by lift.
  • A second funicular to San Vigilio — one more little cable ride up to the castle ruins, just for the thrill of it.

Getting there & the airport run: from Peschiera it's about 2 h with one change — the longest haul of the lot. But the airport run is the payoff: roughly 1h50 to MXP with one change via Milan, still quicker than the dash from Verona. (Bergamo has its own airport, but you fly MXP, so ignore it — the train simply routes you through Milan.)

On the way out · ~2 h to MXP — stay on the lake

Desenzano del Garda

If the thought of leaving the lake is more than anyone can bear, you don't have to — Desenzano is simply the next town west, and it carries a quiet trump card: its own mainline station, Desenzano del Garda–Sirmione, so getaway morning arrives with no transfer friction at all. It's the liveliest place on the south shore, a buzzy harbour promenade where the day winds down over gelato and the slow business of watching boats come and go. There are beaches to swim from, a small castle on the hill, and a Roman villa with mosaics still bright underfoot. After the big days, this is the easy, low-key choice — a soft landing with the water still in view.

  • The harbour promenade & beaches — a last swim and a gelato as the boats drift in and out of the old port.
  • The castle on the hill — a short climb to a small fortress and a parting look back over the lake.
  • The Roman villa — floor mosaics a nine-year-old can walk right up to, picking out the patterns laid down two thousand years ago.

Getting there & the airport run: From Peschiera it's barely a hop west along the lake to Desenzano's own station. To the airport it's about 1 h 52 at the fastest (nearer 2 h 30 typically), with a single change at Milano Centrale onto the Malpensa Express — the simplest stay-on-the-lake launch of all.

A day from the lake

Worth it — but the wrong way for the plane

Three places that earn a day during your Garda block, not a place on getaway morning: a car-free island, and two southern Renaissance cities that are pure wonder for a nine-year-old — just a long, change-heavy haul to the airport if you leave from them.

A day from the lake · the best westward add-on

Lake Iseo & Monte Isola

Slip north off your route at Brescia and Lake Iseo opens up — smaller than Como, quieter than Garda, and somehow still a secret. At its heart sits Monte Isola, the largest lake-island in Europe and entirely car-free, which means no engines, no horns, just footsteps and bicycle bells and the slap of water against the jetties. The lake here is calm and clear and swimmable, the kind of water a nine-year-old wades into without a second thought. Come for the morning, picnic on a pebble shore, and let the day go slow.

  • The little ferry from Sulzano — a five-minute crossing that feels like a proper boat adventure, with another one back every quarter hour.
  • Bikes round the shoreline — rent on the island and ride a stretch of the flat lakeside loop; a nine-year-old can manage a good piece of it.
  • A swim and a picnic — the water is quiet and gentle, far emptier than Como, made for a long lazy afternoon.

Getting there & the airport run: From Peschiera it's about 20 minutes to Brescia, then a Trenord regional on to Sulzano — roughly 1h15–1h30 all told — where a 5–10 minute ferry (every 15–20 minutes) carries you across to the island. The Franciacorta sparkling-wine vineyards next door make an easy adults-with-a-car add-on. As a last-night base it's change-heavy — 2h30+ to MXP — so do it as a day trip and sleep back in Desenzano.

A day from the lake · a southern detour

Mantua

Let's be clear-eyed: Mantua lies south, reached by way of Verona, so it belongs to your Garda days rather than your road to the airport. But what a side-trip it makes. This is a Gonzaga city wrapped on three sides by lakes, a place of dukes and painters where the streets seem to hold their breath. For a nine-year-old it delivers the rare, genuine wow — ceilings that trick the eye and walls that come tumbling down around you.

  • Palazzo Ducale — the Camera degli Sposi — Mantegna's painted ceiling, where cherubs peer down at you through a fake opening to the sky.
  • Palazzo Te — the Hall of the Giants — the whole room wraps you in collapsing, tumbling giants; under-11s enter free.
  • The three lakes — lakeside paths to wander and a little boat trip to round off the day.

Getting there & the airport run: From Peschiera it's about 2 h via Verona — a proper detour rather than a stepping-stone. As an airport leg it's awkward: 3 h-plus and two changes to MXP, so treat Mantua as a day out from the lake, not a last night on the way to the plane.

A day from the lake · a southern detour

Cremona

A southern detour, this one — a day out rather than an airport stop. Cremona is the world capital of violin-making, the city where Stradivari and Guarneri bent maple and spruce into the most coveted instruments ever made, a craft now written into the UNESCO heritage lists. The wonderful surprise is the Museo del Violino, which has no patience for the hushed-glass-case kind of music museum: multimedia rooms, an auditorium where someone actually picks up a priceless violin and plays it, master luthiers shaping wood behind glass, and workshops made for small hands. Then you climb for the view, and come down for nougat and gelato.

  • Museo del Violino — live playing demonstrations, luthiers at work behind glass, and children's workshops; a music museum a nine-year-old won't want to leave.
  • The Torrazzo — one of Europe's tallest brick bell towers; climb it for the payoff view across the rooftops and the Po plain.
  • Torrone & gelato — Cremona invented the local nougat, so the reward at the bottom of the tower is written into the town itself.

Getting there & the airport run: from Peschiera it's about 1h45–2h with one change; Trenord even sells a "Gite in Treno" combo that bundles the regional train with museum entry. To MXP it's a long ~2h16–2h36 — fine for a day out, but a stretch for a getaway morning.
